Safe Environment

A safe environment creates a space for people to openly share their thoughts without being on the defence/ attack.

What is a safe environment?

A safe environment is where people can freely share their opinions, feelings, values, and experiences. Dialogue is freely flowing.

How can you create a safe environment?

To create a safe environment, you need two achieve things: mutual purpose and mutual respect


Mutual purpose means that others perceive that you are working towards a common outcome for the conversation. To create a mutual purpose, you need to show the other person you care about their interests, values, goals, and desires.

Mutual purpose example: You are arranging a meet up with some friends. You suggest going to the cinema, but another freind ignores your message and suggests a shopping trip. Your goal for the conversation is to understand why your friend has made an alternative suggestion and to reach a resolution that suits the both of you. Therefore, instead of blowing off your friends suggestions and forcing your ideas onto the group, you ask your friend why they'd prefer a shopping trip over the cinema. Here you learn your friends intentions (the reason they suggested an alternative option), and you show your friend that you care about their desires (you make an effort to understand why they want what they want).


Part of reaching a mutual purpose for a conversation involves determining what your goal is for the conversation, you can do this by asking yourself two questions before you start the conservation:

  1. What do you want from the conversation? What is your end goal?

    2. For example, I want to have a constructive conversation where others feel safe to share their thoughts.

  2. What don't you want from the conversation?

    3. For example, I don't want the conversation to turn into an argument where someone wins and the other person loses.


Once you have found the mutual purpose, you need to maintain this goal in your head throughout the conversation. You may find your motives change, and by stepping outside of the conversation and looking at yourself from an outsider’s perspective, you can get back on track. To do this, ask yourself what your current behaviour tells you about your motives. Are you pushing for an ulterior motive, a hidden agenda? When you start to push for your agenda/ ulterior motive, you lose sight of mutual purpose/respect and the other person no longer believes you have their best interests at heart, dialogue stops flowing, and the pool of meaning dries up.

Hidden agenda example: Your talking to someone who you think is very smart, so you speak with confidence and make points with more certainty than usual in an effort to try and impress them. This is an example of a hidden agenda, you are trying to come across as more intelligent then you believe you are. As you can see, there are no issues here with safety, the other person is free to share their thoughts and opinions. Where safety becomes a risk is when you push for your personal agenda at the cost of the other person. Say the other person makes a point that contradicts your own, and you respond in a patronising voice and you use hyperbole and exaggeration to enforce your point. The other person no longer feels safe to share their thoughts because they no longer believe you have their best interests at heart or that you respect their opinion. There is no mutual purpose, safety has been lost, and the sharedpool of meaning has dried up.

Mutual respect means that others percieve that you respect their opinion and beliefs. To create this mutual respect, you need to show you are interested in the other persons opinions, this does not mean you have to agree with their opinions.


Without mutual respect, the other person will perceive that you do not respect them and will start defending their dignity, pride, and self-esteem. They will no longer feel safe.


Sometimes it can be difficult to be open to or interested in what someone has to say, especially if you don’t see eye to eye with them.

You might think what they said is stupid, unimportant, but instead of disregarding what they say, you should be curious. Ask yourself questions like why do they think that? What is it that they have experienced in life that led them to form that opinion/value/belief?


If your motive is genuine, the other person will believe your respect them (mutual respect) and that you care about their goals, interests, and values (mutual purpose). To learn about hidden agendas and whether you may have an ulterior motive during conversations, click here.

If you find yourself pushing for a hidden agenda, refocus your brain by asking yourself:

  • What do I really want for myself?
  • What do I really want for others?
  • What do I really want for my relationship?

Then ask yourself how you would behave if you really did want these results.

How do you maintain a safe environment?

  1. Learn to look

    2. Look for issues with safety, when people feel unsafe they resort to two types of defence in response to the fear they feel.

    Silence

    Masking- sarcasm and sugar coating

    Avoiding- steering the attention away from the sensitive topic

    Withdrawing- pulling out of the conversation altogether


    Violence

    Controlling- cutting others off, speaking in absolutes, using hyperboles, and using directive questions

    Labelling- name-calling

    Attacking- threatening




  2. Make it safe
Apologise

Apologize to take back your action that has offended them.
Apologize sincerely if you have made a mistake that has hurt the other person.
When your behaviour has given someone clear cause to doubt your respect then the mutual respect will be lost.
You need to acknowledge your wrongdoing, give up winning, focus on what you really want, and give up a bit of your ego.
A sincere show of respect will help restore the mutual respect that has been lost.
Note that a 'but' after an apology isn't a genuine apology, by justifying/ excusing your actions you are displacing responsibility and being insincere.

Apologizing is accepting your wrongdoing and taking back your actions, unlike contrasting which isn't used to take back what you have said or done but rather to clarify the meaning behind your words/ actions.

Contrast

Contrast to fix the misunderstanding.
Sometimes people will be offended or upset even though you haven't done anything to cause such offence.
This happens when the other person misinterprets the meaning behind your words.
In this instance, you are not 'in the wrong' and it would be inappropriate to apologize.
Rather, you use contrasting to clarify your meaning.
If you don't clarify your meaning then the other person may believe that your intention is to win the argument/ coerce them to accept your opinion, and they will no longer believe that you care about their goals, interests, and values (mutual purpose and respect are lost).
Rebuild mutual purpose by contrasting what you do mean with what you don’t mean.

What you don’t mean: Address the other persons concerns that you have malicious intent or don’t respect them
What you do mean: Clarify your real purpose

Example
Your friend is visiting the UK who you haven't seen for a year, and so you plan to see them instead of seeing your mum that weekend.
While this is completely reasonable to do, your mother gets upset because she thinks you don't want to spend time with her.
Here, you wouldn't apologize, rather you would use contrasting to clarify that it is not that you don't want to see your mum, it is that you haven't seen this friend for a very long time and that this might be the only opportunity you have to see your friend for a year or two.
You haven't changed your plan to see your friend (you don't take back your actions), rather you have clarified your meaning.
You could then say to your mum you will come down the next weekend to see her, this way you are demonstrating your meaning is true (that it isn't that you don't want to see her) while not taking back your original plan of action.
CRIB

You might find yourself in the middle of a debate because you and the other person have different purposes (no mutual purpose).
Contrasting won’t do the trick here because you will be reinstating a goal that is not shared with the other person and it will therefore not restore mutual purpose.
Instead, you need to use CRIB.

Commit

to seeking a mutual purpose

You have to accept that your belief might not be the best one, your mind must be open to the possibility that there are better alternatives.

For example, you have just received a job offer from a company in another country and you are trying to convince your wife that it would be a good idea to move the family so that you can take the job. Ask yourself, before trying to coerce your wife to your idea, is that job for that particular company the only thing that's going to make you happy? Why that particular company? What makes them better than everyone else? Once you have opened your mind to alternative suggestions you can commit yourself to continue discussions until a mutual purpose is found.

Commit to finding mutual purpose: "It seems we have a different opinion on this, how about we take no action on it until we have found a solution that suits us both?"

Recognise

the purpose behind the strategy

We equate what we are asking for with what we want, but in reality, what we ask for is just a strategy to get what we want. You need to find out the reason behind the strategy: what it is the person wants, not what the person is asking for.

For example, Katie wants to go out for cocktails, but her partner Ben wants to stay in and watch a movie. These two activities are incompatible. Katie asks her partner why he wants to stay in and watch a movie, and he says he is tired and wants to have a chilled evening with her. The purpose behind Bens strategy is to have a chilled evening, and the purpose behind Katie's strategy is to get away from the kids for a bit. So why not find an activity that is both relaxing and is also away from the kids? While the activities are not compatible, the goals are. They could get sucked into arguing over which activity to do, but instead, they find out the purpose behind the strategy and then brainstorm ideas for activities that satisfy both their needs, such as a drive through movie night (which is relaxing, depending on the movie they choose, and it gets them away from the kids).

Find out their purpose, ask them why they want what they are asking for, and use this understanding of the other persons wants/ needs to create a mutual purpose.

Invent

a mutual purpose

Sometimes it may be that your goals aren't compatible; it may be that your wants cannot be served, except at the expense of the other person. In this case, you are going to need to a little creativity and invent a mutual purpose. To invent a mutual purpose you need to zoom out and look at longer-term goals to find a goal that is more meaningful than the goals that are diving the various sides apart.

For example, your partner has been offered a job that is located 3 hours away from where you both currently live. You want him to decline the job offer because if he accepts it he would have to move away, but he wants to accept it because he thinks he would enjoy the job and he likes the company. The short term goals are incompatible, but they can both agree on the long-term goal which is that the needs of their relationship come before career goals.

Brainstorm

new strategies

Once a mutual purpose has been found or invented, safety will be restored and you can move back into dialogue and brainstorm new ideas that will meet the needs of everyone. It is important that everyone agrees on the final goal and works towards it because it will stop people from passively resisting the outcome.
